📖 About the Camino de Santiago

Understanding the Ancient Pilgrimage Route

The Ancient Route

The Camino de Santiago, also known as the Way of St. James, is one of the oldest pilgrimage routes in the world. For over 1,000 years, millions of pilgrims have walked this sacred path across northern Spain toward the Cathedral of Santiago de Compostela in Galicia. Legend says that the remains of the Apostle St. James are buried beneath the cathedral, making it one of the most important pilgrimage sites in Christianity.

The route was officially recognized by UNESCO as a World Heritage Site in 1993, acknowledging its profound cultural and spiritual significance. Today, it continues to attract walkers from every corner of the globe—pilgrims seeking spiritual enlightenment, adventurers challenging themselves physically, and travelers hungry for cultural immersion.

Why Walk the Camino?

🧘 Spiritual Journey

Whether you're religious or not, the Camino offers a space for reflection, meditation, and personal growth. Many walkers report transformative experiences on the route.

🏃 Physical Challenge

Push yourself physically and discover your own strength and resilience. The Camino tests your body but also shows you what you're truly capable of achieving.

🌟 Self-Discovery

Away from the distractions of daily life, the Camino provides time for introspection. Many pilgrims find answers to important life questions on the trail.

🌍 Global Connection

Meet people from dozens of countries. The Camino creates a unique sense of community among walkers from all walks of life and cultures.

🎓 Cultural Learning

Experience authentic Spanish culture, regional cuisine, and centuries-old traditions. Stay in small towns and learn from locals along the way.

📸 Adventure & Nature

Explore diverse Spanish landscapes—from rolling hills to mountain passes. Every day brings new terrain and breathtaking natural beauty.

The Route We're Walking

Our 5-day journey covers approximately 80-85 kilometers, starting from a strategic point in northern Spain and following the historic Camino Francés (French Route), the most popular pilgrimage path. This route takes us through the Basque Country and into Galicia.

Did You Know?

⭐ The Scallop Shell

The scallop shell (venera) is the universal symbol of the Camino. Pilgrims wear or carry it to identify themselves and as protection on their journey.

📝 The Credential

Every pilgrim receives a credential (pasaporte del peregrino) that's stamped at accommodations and churches along the way—your personal record of the journey.

🥾 Yellow Arrows

The route is marked with yellow arrows and scallop shell symbols painted on walls, roads, and trees. These guides have helped pilgrims find their way for centuries.
