💧 Water & Hydration Logistics

Finding and Carrying Water on the Camino

Managing your drinking water on the Sarria to Santiago stretch is straightforward because the infrastructure for pilgrims is highly developed.

Carrying Capacity

You do not need to carry heavy, multi-liter hydration bladders. Carrying 1 to 1.5 liters at a time is entirely sufficient. The route passes through a village, hamlet, or cafe every 3 to 5 kilometers, meaning you will have constant opportunities to refill. A standard reusable water bottle or a lightweight collapsible flask works best.

Pro Tip

A lightweight collapsible water bottle (they pack down to nothing when empty) is ideal. When empty, it weighs almost nothing. When full, it's the same as a rigid bottle—but saves space in your pack.

Public Fountains (Fuentes)

Along the trail and in the center of almost every village square, you will find public stone fountains built specifically for pilgrims and locals.

✓ How to Use Them

  • Most fountains have a long spout for filling bottles
  • Fill at the source where water flows directly out
  • Wait for locals to fill first if there's a line (pilgrims have priority, but be respectful)
  • Always check for signs indicating water safety

Safety and Signage

The tap water and public fountain water in Galicia is safe to drink, but you must pay attention to the signs on the outdoor fountains:

✅ Agua Potable

The water is treated and safe for drinking. This is the sign you want to see!

❌ Agua No Potable (or Sin Garantía Sanitaria)

The water is untreated (often agricultural or straight from a local spring without testing). Do not drink from these.

❓ No Sign At All

If a fountain has no sign at all, it is safest to skip it and wait for the next village.

Cafes and Bars

Whenever you stop for a coffee or a bathroom break, you can ask the staff to refill your bottle from the tap by asking for "agua del grifo". Bottled water is also sold at every cafe, supermarket, and vending machine along the route for about €1.

Spanish Tip: Say "¿Puedo rellenar mi botella de agua del grifo, por favor?" (Can I refill my water bottle with tap water, please?) or "Uno coffee por favor" (One coffee please xD)

Most cafe owners are very accommodating and happy to help pilgrims—many businesses have become accustomed to this request.

Daily Hydration Strategy

Morning (Before 8:00 AM)

Start with a full bottle. Drink a cup of coffee (counts as hydration!) at breakfast. Fill up before leaving your accommodation.

Mid-Morning (10:30 AM - 11:00 AM)

Stop at a cafe for a snack and refill your bottle. This is your second breakfast break and hydration checkpoint.

Afternoon (1:00 PM - 2:00 PM)

During your lunch break, refill your bottle. You'll typically have 1-1.5 hours before the afternoon leg. This is plenty of time to drink and refill.

Final Stretch (3:00 PM - 6:00 PM)

You should finish walking by 5-6 PM. Fill up one last time mid-afternoon to carry you through to dinner.

Evening

Drink plenty of water with dinner and before bed. Your body needs to recover and hydrate overnight.

Water Access by Daily Stop

Here's what to expect at each major town along your route:

Sarria (Start)

Large town with multiple cafes and supermarkets. Plenty of water access before you begin.

Portomarín

Significant stop with cafes, restaurants, and public fountains. Water is readily available.

Palas de Rei

Medium-sized town with good cafe and fountain access throughout.

Arzúa

Popular stop with multiple cafes and a central plaza fountain. Excellent water infrastructure.

O Pedrouzo

Small town but built for pilgrims. Several cafes and visible water sources.

Santiago de Compostela

City with abundant water access everywhere. You'll never lack for water on arrival.

Trail Water Access Between Towns

  • Villages: Almost every hamlet has a public fountain or a cafe where you can ask for water.
  • Monastery stops: Many monasteries have pilgrim fountains specifically for this purpose.
  • Farm stands: Rural vendors sometimes sell drinks (water, juice, soda) at tiny roadside stands.
  • Streams and springs: You'll see small natural springs marked along the trail. If marked "agua potable" by locals, they're safe to drink from—but stick to fountains and cafes if you're unsure.

Dehydration Signs & Prevention

Watch for These Signs of Dehydration:

  • Dark yellow urine (should be pale yellow)
  • Dry mouth or lips
  • Dizziness or lightheadedness
  • Fatigue beyond normal hiking tiredness
  • Headache that doesn't respond to pain relief

Prevention: Drink before you're thirsty. A good rule: if your urine is yellow, drink more water. Aim for drinking 200-300 ml (6-10 oz) every 20 minutes during active hiking.

Electrolyte Balance

On hot days or if you're sweating heavily, consider bringing electrolyte powder packets (like Liquid IV or similar). These are lightweight and add essential minerals (sodium, potassium, magnesium) that plain water doesn't provide. You can mix them into water at cafes.

Alternatively, eating salty snacks (cured ham, olives, nuts) and drinking soup at lunch naturally replaces electrolytes.

Bottom Line

✓ Water is not a concern on this route. The infrastructure is excellent, villages are frequent, and safe drinking water is everywhere. The most important thing is to stay hydrated by drinking consistently throughout the day—before you feel thirsty. Dehydration happens gradually and often goes unnoticed until it becomes a problem. Drink early and drink often.
